1 Bayes theorem

Let \(A\) be the event that it rains next week and \(B\) the event that the weather forecaster predicts that there will be rain next week. Let’s suppose that the probability of rain next week is \(\mathbb{P}(A) = 0.9\). Suppose also that the conditional probability that there is a forecast of rain, given that it really does rain, is \(\mathbb{P}(B\mid{A}) = 0.8\). On the other hand, the conditional probability that there is a forecast of dry weather, given that there really isn’t any rain is \(\mathbb{P}(B^c \mid {A^c}) = 0.75\).

Now suppose that there is a forecast of rain. What is the conditional probability of rain, given the forecast of rain \(\mathbb{P}(A\mid{B})\)?

Answer:

First, we compute \(\mathbb{P}(B)\):

\[\mathbb{P}(B) = \mathbb{P}({B}\cap{A}) + \mathbb{P}({B}\cap{A^c}) = \mathbb{P}({B}\mid{A})\mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}({B}\mid{A^c})\mathbb{P}(A^c) = 0.8 * 0.9 + {1 - \mathbb{P}({B^c}\mid{A^c})}\mathbb{P}(A^c) = 0.72 + 0.025 = 0.745\]

We then apply Bayes theorem:

\[\mathbb{P}({A}\mid{B}) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}({B}\mid{A})}{\mathbb{P}(B}) = \frac{0.9 * 0.8}{0.745} \approx 0.966\]

Suppose we have a probability space (\(\Omega\), \(\varepsilon\), \(\mathbb{P}\)).

(Q1) Suppose that \(A\), \(B\) \(\in\) \(\varepsilon\) and \(A \subseteq B\). Give an expression for \(\mathbb{P}({A}\mid{B})\) in terms of \(P(A)\) and \(P(B)\). What about when \(\mathbb{P}(A \backslash B) = 0\)?

(A1) If \[\mathbb{P}(A \backslash B) = 0\] Then, \[\mathbb{P}(A) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap {B}) + \mathbb{P}(A \cap {B^c}) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap {B}) + \mathbb{P}(A \backslash {B}) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap {B})\]

Hence, \[\mathbb{P}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap {B})}{\mathbb{P}(B)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A)}{\mathbb{P}(B)}\].

(Q2) Suppose that \(A\), \(B\) \(\in\) \(\varepsilon\) with \(A \cap B=\phi\). Give an expression for \(\mathbb{P}({A}\mid{B})\). What about when \(\mathbb{P}(A\cap{B}) = 0\)?

(A2) If \[\mathbb{P}(A \cap {B}) = 0\] Then, \[\mathbb{P}(A \mid {B}) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap {B})}{\mathbb{P}(B) }= 0\]

(Q3) Suppose that \(A\), \(B\) \(\in\) \(\varepsilon\) with \(B \subseteq A\). Give an expression for \(\mathbb{P}(A\mid{B})\). What about when \(\mathbb{P}(B \backslash A) = 0\)?

(A3) If \[\mathbb{P}(B \backslash A) = 0\] Then, \[\mathbb{P}(A^c \cap {B}) = 0\]

Hence, \[\mathbb{P}(B) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap {B}) + \mathbb{P}(A^c \cap {B}) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap {B})\] And, \[\mathbb{P}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap {B})}{\mathbb{P}(B)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(B)}{\mathbb{P}(B)} = 1\]

(Q4) Suppose that \(A \in \varepsilon\). Give an expression for \(\mathbb{P}(A \mid \Omega)\) in terms of \(\mathbb{P}(A)\).

(A4) In this case, we have \(A \subseteq \Omega\) and \(\mathbb{P}(\Omega) = 1\), So, \[\mathbb{P}(A \mid \Omega) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A)}{\mathbb{P}(\Omega)} = \mathbb{P}(A)\]

(A5) \[\mathbb{P}(A \cap {B} \cap {C}) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap (B \cap C) = \mathbb{P}(A \mid (B \cap C)) \cdot \mathbb{P}(B \cap C) = \mathbb{P}(A \mid (B \cap C)) \cdot \mathbb{P}(B \mid C) \cdot \mathbb{P}(C)\]

(Q6) Show that given three events \(A\), \(B\), \(C \in \varepsilon\) we have \(\mathbb{P}(A \mid B \cap C) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(B \mid A \cap C) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A \mid C)}{\mathbb{P}(B \mid C)}\).

(A6) First, we compute the left part of the formula that \[\mathbb{P}(A \mid B \cap C) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap B \cap C)}{\mathbb{P}(B \cap C)}\]

Then, we compute the right part, since that \[\mathbb{P}(A \mid C) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap C)}{\mathbb{P}(C)}\] And, \[\mathbb{P}(B \mid C) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(B \cap C)}{\mathbb{P}(C)}\]

(A)

num_red_balls <- 3
num_total_balls <- 10
total_draws <- 35

prob_red_spheres <- function(z){
  result <- choose(total_draws, z) * 
    (num_red_balls / num_total_balls) ^ z * 
    ((num_total_balls - num_red_balls) / num_total_balls) ^ (total_draws - z)
  return(result)
}

6 The Monty hall problem (*)

Consider the following game:

At a game show there are three seemingly identical doors. Behind one of the doors is a car, and behind the remaining two is a goat. The contestant of the game first gets to choose one of the three doors. The host then opens one of the other two doors to reveal a goat. The contestant now get’s a chance to either (a) switch their choice to the other unopened door or (b) stick to their original choice. The host then opens the door corresponding to the contestant’s final choice. They get to keep whatever is behind their final choice of door.

Does the contestant improve their chances of winning the car if they switch their choice? For clarity, we make the following assumptions:

  1. The car is assigned to one of the doors at random with equal probability for each door.
  2. The assignment of the car and the initial choice of the contestant are independent.
  3. Once the contestant makes their initial choice, the host always opens a door which (a) has a goat behind it and (b) is not the contestant’s initial choice. If there is more than one such door (i.e. when the contestant’s initial choice corresponds to the door with a car behind it) the host chooses at random from the two possibilities with equal probability.

To formalise our problem we introduce the following events for \(i\) = 1, 2, 3:

  • \(A_i\) denotes event car is placed behind the \(i\)-th door;
  • \(B_i\) denotes event contestant initially chooses the \(i\)-th door;
  • \(C_i\) denotes event the host opens the \(i\)-th door to reveal a goat.

(Q) Consider a situation in which the contestant initially selects the first door (\(B1\)) and then the host opens the second door to reveal a goat (\(C2\)). What is \(\mathbb{P}(A3 \mid B1 \cap C2)\)?

(A) We first compute \(\mathbb{P}(A_3 \mid B_1 \cap C_2)\), which is: \[ \mathbb{P}(A_3 \mid B_1 \cap C_2) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \cap A_3 \cap B_1)}{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \cap B_1)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_3 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_3 \cap B_1)}{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \cap B_1)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_3 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_3 \mid B_1)}{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid B_1)} \] And we shall use the above formula.

To evaluate this expression we first need to compute \(\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid B_1)\):

\(\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid B_1) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \cap B_1)}{\mathbb{P}(B_1)}\)

\(\qquad \qquad \; = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A_1 \cap C_2 \cap B_1) + \mathbb{P}(A_2 \cap C_2 \cap B_1) + \mathbb{P}(A_3 \cap C_2 \cap B_1)}{\mathbb{P}(B_1)}\)

\(\qquad \qquad \; = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A_1 \mid C_2 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_1 \cap B_1) + \mathbb{P}(A_2 \mid C_2 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_2 \cap B_1) + \mathbb{P}(A_3 \mid C_2 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_3 \cap B_1)}{\mathbb{P}(B_1)}\)

\(\qquad \qquad \; = \mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_1 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_1) + \mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_2 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_2) + \mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_3 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_3)\)

\(\qquad \qquad \; = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_1 \cap B_1) + \mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_2 \cap B_1) + \mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_3 \cap B_1)\}\)

\(\qquad \qquad \; = \frac{1}{3} \cdot (\frac{1}{2} + 0 + 1) = \frac{1}{2}\)

And we have \(\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_3 \cap B_1) = 1\),

By using the independence of the choice of the door for the car and the contestnt’s initial choice, combined with the fact that the initial assignment of the car is random with equal probabilities, we have \(\mathbb{P}(A_3 \mid B_1) = \mathbb{P}(A_3) = \frac{1}{3}\).

Hence, we have \[ \mathbb{P}(A_3 \mid B_1 \cap C_2) = = \frac{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid A_3 \cap B_1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(A_3 \mid B_1)}{\mathbb{P}(C_2 \mid B_1)} = \frac{1 \cdot \frac{1}{3}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{2}{3} \]

(Q) What does this suggest about a good strategy? Should we switch choices?

(A) By the same argument we obtain \(\mathbb{P}(A_{i_3} \mid B_{i_1} \cap C_{i_2}) = \frac{2}{3}\) for any \(i_1, i_2, i_3 \in \{1,2,3\}\) such that \(\{i_1,i_2,i_3\} = \{1,2,3\}\). Hence, the contestant always increases their chances by switching choice (from \(i_1\) to \(i_3\)).

7 A game of marbles (**)

This question is left as an optional challenge for those who have completed all remaining questions.

Suppose there two players - \(A\) and \(B\). Player \(A\) has \(n\) marbles and player \(B\) has \(k − n\) marbles \((n \leq k)\), so the two players have \(k\) marbles between them. The game proceeds through a series of rounds. In each round \(A\) has probability \(p\) of winning the round, and \(B\) has probability \(1 − p\) of winning the round, where \(p \in (0, 1/2)\) is a fixed number. If \(A\) wins a round then \(B\) must give \(A\) one of his marbles. If \(B\) wins the round then \(A\) must give \(B\) one of his marbles. All of the rounds are independent from one another. A player wins the entire game of marbles when they have all \(k\) marbles, and the other player has none.

(Q) Can you give a formula for the probability that player \(A\) wins the game in terms of \(n\) and \(k\) and \(p\)?
